Version 1.0, February 2026. A living document. Revisit and update monthly.

## The Core Thesis

You are not a developer who discovered strategy. You are not an Ivy League credential looking for a title. You are something rarer and more valuable: a consultant who spent a decade learning how humans adopt technology at scale, and then went inside the machine to understand AI from the product side.

Your background in HR, organizational psychology, retail field operations, and creator economy strategy is not a gap. It is the exact lens that AI companies are missing when they wonder why their tools don't get adopted, why their product concepts don't land with users, and why their research doesn't translate into features that matter.

One-line positioning: "I help AI companies build products that humans actually use, because I've spent 10 years studying the human side of technology adoption."

---

## I. Positioning Tenets

These are not just resume lines. They are the beliefs you embody and demonstrate consistently, in your projects, your content, your conversations, and eventually your work.

---

### 1. People-First AI

Technology adoption is a human problem before it is a technical problem. You have sat across from retail associates in 100 or more stores, watched creators fumble through mobile prototypes, and translated field-level friction into product strategy for executives. You know that the most sophisticated AI feature fails if it doesn't fit how humans actually work.

How you embody it: Every project you build asks "who is this for and what do they already know?" before it asks "what can the AI do?"

---

### 2. Practitioner Over Pundit

You don't talk about AI tools; you build with them. Agents, workflows, prompting systems, motion design, music generators: your work exists as artifacts, not slide decks. In a world full of AI commentators, your credibility comes from shipping.

How you embody it: Every LinkedIn post you write is anchored to something you actually built or tested. No hot takes without receipts.

---

### 3. The Translator

You sit at the intersection of two worlds that rarely speak well to each other: the language of executive strategy (ROI, adoption, stakeholder buy-in, product-market fit) and the language of technical execution (agents, models, prompting, workflows). You are the person who can sit in both rooms.

How you embody it: Your public projects are always paired with strategic framing, not just "here's what I built" but "here's why this matters and what decision it helps make."

---

### 4. The Non-Traditional Path Is the Point

You did not arrive at AI through a CS degree or a bootcamp. You arrived through a decade of watching how people relate to products, in stores, in apps, in creative workflows. That path gave you something technical practitioners often lack: empathy for the non-expert user.

How you embody it: You tell this story explicitly and often. Your LinkedIn content is in part a roadmap for people like you, business thinkers, creatives, and operators, who want to contribute to AI without fitting the default mold.

---

### 5. Systems Over One-Offs

You don't just use AI; you design systems that produce consistent, repeatable outputs. Your instinct is to build the workflow before you build the output. This is what separates strategic AI thinking from casual AI use.

How you embody it: The projects you build publicly are always documented as systems, with the logic, the workflow, and the decision-making exposed, not just the final output.

## II. Two-Track Career Strategy

You are managing two parallel realities. This is not indecision; it is sophisticated portfolio thinking applied to your own career. Each track informs the other.

---

### Track A: US Short-Term Runway (Active Now through 6 Months)

Goal: Secure a role or engagement that extends your TN visa status, generates income, and buys time for Track B preparation.

TN Visa Reality Check: Your TN visa category as "Management Consultant" is one of the most flexible and renewable TN classifications. It does not require employer-specific sponsorship the way H-1B does; you need a qualifying US employer or agency arrangement and a letter of support. This matters for how you approach the market.

#### 1. Agency-to-Client Model (Lowest Friction)

Another arrangement like Blinkk or Magnit, or a similar staffing and consulting firm that places you at a tech client, is the path of least resistance for TN continuity. Target agencies include Magnit, Insight Global, Staffmark, TEKsystems, and boutique AI and product strategy consultancies.

#### 2. Direct FTE at a Tech Company (Most Stability)

Many tech companies can sponsor TN visas directly for management consultant or strategy roles. They do not petition with USCIS; you just need the employer support letter and can cross at the border. Target companies include AI-native firms such as Runway, ElevenLabs, Adobe, Snap, and Netflix, as well as larger tech companies with active AI divisions. Title targets: AI Product Strategist, AI Tools Consultant, Creator Partnerships and Strategy, Product Marketing Manager (AI), AI Strategy and Insights.

#### 3. Remote Contract via Canadian Entity (Maximum Flexibility)

If you register as a sole proprietor or incorporate in Canada (Ontario), you can legally contract with US companies remotely as a Canadian business. This is a legal grey area for TN purposes but viable as a bridge strategy, especially if paired with a plan to be in Canada or the Philippines part of the time. Consult a cross-border tax and immigration attorney before executing.

Target role types for Track A: AI Product Strategist and AI Insights and Strategy, AI Tools Consultant and Fractional AI Advisor, Creator Strategy and AI Integration, Product Marketing Manager for AI Products, and AI Research and Synthesis (qualitative).

Week 1 priorities:

| Identify 3 former colleagues or managers for warm referrals | At target companies, not just former agencies |
| Target 10 specific companies and find the actual hiring manager | Not just the job posting |

### Track B: Remote Relocation Preparation (Parallel, 3 to 12 Months)

Goal: Build the infrastructure, professional, legal, and financial, to work remotely as a consultant and solo practitioner from Japan or the Philippines.

#### Philippines

The fastest path given your heritage. If you are pursuing dual Filipino citizenship (which your research notes confirm is in progress), you gain the right to live and work there without visa restrictions. As a Canadian and Filipino citizen, you can run a freelance and consulting practice from Manila, Cebu, or remotely with minimal immigration friction. Cost of living gives you significant runway.

#### Japan

Requires a digital nomad or "Specified Skilled Worker" or business manager visa path. Japan launched a digital nomad visa in 2024; it allows stays of up to 6 months, renewable, for remote workers earning above a qualifying threshold. Verify current requirements. Given your interest in Japanese culture and prior research into Tokyo as an AI hub, this is worth investigating seriously as a 12-month target.

#### What You Need to Build for Track B

Track B requires five things running in parallel. First, a billable consulting offer: a defined service that clients can hire you for remotely, which the proof-of-work projects in Part IV feed directly into. Second, a Canadian business entity to invoice clients internationally and maintain clean tax residency. Third, a public portfolio so clients can find you without a referral. Fourth, a cross-border financial setup including Canadian and US accounts and international wire and payment capabilities through services like Wise and Stripe. Fifth, completed immigration research covering the Philippine dual citizenship timeline and Japan digital nomad visa specifics.

Track B does not require you to quit Track A. The goal is to build the freelance infrastructure in parallel so that when Track A naturally ends, or when you choose to leave, you have a runway already generating income.

## III. Reference Strategy

This section covers who can speak for you, how to cultivate those relationships, and what you need them to say without breaching NDAs.

---

### The NDA-Constrained Reference Problem

You cannot ask anyone to describe specific Google project details. But references can legitimately speak to how you think and approach problems, what it is like to work with you in a consulting context, your communication style and strategic instincts, the type of work you did (for example, "AI product strategy for a major tech client") without naming deliverables, and your trajectory and growth as a practitioner.

---

### Tier 1: The Strategic Validator

Former stakeholders or managers who can speak to your seniority and impact. From your Google work, anyone who was a client-side stakeholder (not agency-side) and can speak to the quality of your strategic output. They do not need to name projects; they just need to confirm your level and approach.

How to ask: "I'm in a transition and looking for roles at the intersection of AI strategy and product. Would you be comfortable being a reference? I'd make sure any conversation stays at the level of approach and methodology rather than specifics."

---

### Tier 2: The Peer Validator

Consultants or colleagues who worked alongside you and can speak to your technical AI fluency and systems thinking: peers from your Google engagements or the broader LA and tech consulting community who witnessed your work style directly.

---

### Tier 3: The Legacy Validator

People from your pre-NDA career, Twitter (2016) and Beats by Dr. Dre (2016 to 2021). These are clean references with no NDA concerns. The Beats engagement in particular has a strong outcome story: $100M revenue, 100 or more retailers, a 21-person team. Reconnect now.

---

### Tier 4: The Character and Thought Partnership Validator

People who know how you think: mentors, collaborators, creative partners. As you build your public profile, people who have engaged with your LinkedIn content or collaborated on projects, even informally, can speak to your point of view and intellectual contribution.

Week 2 outreach priorities:

| Action | Notes |
|---|---|
| Identify 5 specific people across Tiers 1 to 3 | Name them; don't leave this abstract |
| Send personalized reconnection messages | Lead with genuine connection, then mention the transition; don't open with the ask |
| Brief each reference on your current positioning | So they can speak consistently about where you're headed |

## IV. Proof-of-Work Project Roadmap

These projects are designed to be built publicly, demonstrate your positioning tenets, and create artifacts you can share with employers, clients, and your LinkedIn audience. They are sequenced by build time and strategic value.

| Project | Timeline | Strategic Value |
|---|---|---|
| "The Strategy Brief Agent" | Week 1 to 2 | Translates your core consulting skill into a public tool |
| "The Non-Traditional Path to AI" Playbook | Week 2 to 3 | Becomes your thought leadership anchor and content engine |
| "AI Tool Teardowns" Series | Ongoing from Week 2 | Demonstrates product eye; creates consistent content |
| "Creator Insights Synthesizer" Agent | Month 2 | Maps to your Google work scope without naming it |
| "How I Build" Series | Month 2 onwards | Humanizes technical work; proves you are actually doing it |

---

### 1. "The Strategy Brief Agent" (Week 1 to 2)

What it is: An AI agent that takes raw research notes, user quotes, or scattered observations and synthesizes them into a structured executive strategy brief with a clear insight, so-what, and recommended action.

Why it's right for you: It is literally what you do for work, abstracted into a public tool. It demonstrates AI agent architecture without exposing any client work, is immediately useful to other consultants, and positions you as someone who automates strategic thinking rather than just content creation.

Build stack: Claude API or OpenAI with a simple interface such as Streamlit, Notion, or a documented prompt system. Document the entire workflow publicly.

Deliverable: A working agent paired with a LinkedIn post explaining the design decisions behind it, not just "I built a thing," but the strategic choices that shaped it.

---

### 2. "The Non-Traditional Path to AI" Notion Playbook (Week 2 to 3)

What it is: A free, public Notion document or simple website that maps the path from business, creative, and operations backgrounds into AI strategy roles. Structured as: your story, the transferable skills, the learning path, the portfolio, and the positioning.

Why it's right for you: It is your story, which means you are not fabricating thought leadership; you are documenting it. It creates organic LinkedIn content for weeks, with each section becoming a post. It builds an audience of people who will refer and hire you because they trust your perspective, and differentiates you clearly from the "I just passed my AI certification" crowd.

Deliverable: A published Notion document and a launch post that specifically names "this is what no one told me when I was trying to break in."

---

### 3. "AI Tool Teardowns" Series (Ongoing from Week 2)

What it is: A regular LinkedIn series where you pick an AI tool and analyze it through a product strategy lens, not "here are the features" but "here's who this is actually built for, what the design assumptions reveal about the product philosophy, and what I'd change."

Why it's right for you: It demonstrates your product eye, not just AI fluency. It positions you as someone who evaluates tools like a practitioner and buyer rather than a fan, creates consistent content with a defined format, and attracts hiring managers who recognize strategic thinking when they see it.

First teardown candidates: Runway, LTX Studio, ElevenLabs, and Glif.app, all tools you already use and have opinions about.

---

### 4. "Creator Insights Synthesizer" Agent (Month 2)

What it is: A public tool that ingests creator feedback from YouTube comments, Twitter and X replies, and Reddit threads, then synthesizes recurring themes into a product insight report, mirroring the research synthesis work you've done professionally.

Why it's right for you: It maps directly to your Google work scope without naming it, demonstrates domain expertise in the creator economy and AI, is useful enough that creators and product teams might actually use it, and can be packaged as a case study in your portfolio.

---

### 5. "How I Build" Series (Month 2 Onwards)

What it is: A behind-the-scenes LinkedIn series documenting your agent-building process, not polished tutorials but honest process notes covering what you tried, what broke, what you learned, and what surprised you.

Why it's right for you: It humanizes the technical work in a way that fits your non-traditional path narrative, resonates with a large audience of people who are also figuring this out, and builds trust through honesty. The imperfections are the point; they prove you are actually doing the work.

## V. LinkedIn Thought Leadership Plan

---

### Your Content Identity

The person you are on LinkedIn: the AI strategist with a consulting backbone and a human lens, who tells the truth about what it's actually like to break into AI from a non-technical background, and who backs every opinion with something they built.

---

### Content Pillars

### 1. The Non-Traditional Path (40% of content)

Stories and frameworks for people coming from business, creative, and operations backgrounds. This is your highest-differentiation territory. No one with your exact combination of consulting, creator economy, and AI builder experience is telling this story publicly.

Example post angles: "I have no CS degree. Here's how I ended up doing AI product strategy at Google." "The skill I used most in AI work? It wasn't prompting. It was this." "Why your HR background might be your biggest AI advantage."

### 2. Practitioner Proof (30% of content)

Project launches, tool builds, agent walkthroughs, always with strategic framing. Not just "I built this" but "here's the problem it solves and the design decision behind it."

### 3. AI Tool and Product POV (20% of content)

Teardowns, opinions, observations. Positioned as a practitioner evaluating tools, not a reviewer rating them.

### 4. The Human Side (10% of content)

Observations about AI adoption, organizational change, and what gets lost when we move too fast. This is where your HR and psychology background earns authority. Use sparingly; it's the depth card.

---

### Posting Cadence and Engagement

Post 3 times per week to start, prioritizing quality over quantity. Monday is for story and narrative posts from Pillars 1 or 4. Wednesday is for practitioner proof from Pillar 2. Friday is for POV and opinion from Pillar 3.

For engagement, comment thoughtfully on 5 posts per day from: AI product managers, creator economy leaders, non-technical AI practitioners, and LA-based tech community members. The goal is to be recognized as a consistent, smart voice before you are known for a specific product or project.

## Appendix: The NDA-Safe Case Study Format

When you reference your Google work in interviews, on LinkedIn, or in proposals, use this framing:

> "I spent three years embedded with a major technology platform's AI product team, conducting qualitative research with creators, synthesizing behavioral insights, and building high-fidelity prototypes to demonstrate AI automation opportunities. The specifics are confidential, but I can speak to the methodology, the decision-making process, and the skills developed."

Then pivot immediately to a proof-of-work project that demonstrates the same capability publicly. The NDA work establishes credibility; the public work proves it.
